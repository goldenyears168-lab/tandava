Your schedule is the single most important product your studio ships. It decides
who walks through the door, which teachers thrive, and whether your space earns
its rent. Yet most studios build it by inertia — copying last season and adding a
class wherever someone asks.

Here's a more deliberate approach.

## Start from demand, not availability

The most common scheduling mistake is starting with *when teachers are free* and
hoping students show up. Flip it. Begin with the times your community already
wants to move:

- **Early morning (6–8am):** commuters and pre-work practitioners.
- **Midday (12–1pm):** lunch-break classes, often shorter formats.
- **Evening (5:30–7:30pm):** your highest-demand window almost everywhere.
- **Weekend mornings:** longer, slower, community-oriented sessions.

Map your strongest formats onto those windows first. Fill the edges afterward.

## Protect a consistent "anchor" grid

Students book what they can remember. A schedule where Vinyasa is *always*
Monday at 6pm builds a habit; one that moves weekly breaks it. Pick a handful of
anchor classes and keep them fixed for an entire season, even if early
attendance is soft. Consistency compounds.

## Balance the load across teachers

A schedule is also a staffing plan. Watch for two failure modes:

1. **Burnout clustering** — one teacher carrying every prime-time slot.
2. **Thin coverage** — a format that dies the week its only teacher is sick.

Aim for at least two teachers fluent in each core format, and cap prime-time
slots per teacher per week so your strongest people don't quietly exhaust
themselves.

## Make booking effortless

Once the grid is set, the goal is zero friction:

- Show real-time spots remaining so popular classes create urgency.
- Let students book a week out — long enough to plan, short enough to commit.
- Offer a clear waitlist so a full class still captures intent.

## Review every four to six weeks

Treat the schedule as a living system. Each cycle, look at attendance per slot,
no-show rates, and waitlist depth. Promote the classes with waitlists into bigger
rooms or extra sessions; quietly retire the slots that never fill. Small,
frequent adjustments beat a dramatic seasonal overhaul.

A schedule that fills itself isn't luck — it's the result of matching real demand
to consistent times, supported by teachers who aren't stretched thin. Build it
that way and booking becomes the easy part.
