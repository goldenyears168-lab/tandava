Most studios obsess over getting new students through the door. But the math of a
healthy studio is built on retention: keeping an existing member is several times
cheaper than winning a new one, and loyal members refer the next wave for free.

These five levers move retention without a marketing budget.

## 1. Nail the first three weeks

A member's decision to stay is largely made in their first month. Build a
deliberate onboarding:

- A welcome message within 24 hours of their first class.
- A nudge toward a *second* class within their first week.
- A check-in after three or four visits.

The goal is to get a new student to a habit-forming cadence before motivation
fades.

## 2. Watch for the silent drop-off

The most valuable signal you have is *absence*. A member who came twice a week
and hasn't booked in ten days is at risk — and a short, human message at that
moment often brings them back. Don't wait for the membership to lapse; intervene
while the habit is only paused, not broken.

## 3. Make memberships the obvious choice

Drop-ins are convenient for students but volatile for you. Price and frame
memberships so that a regular practitioner would feel silly *not* having one:

- Position the membership against two or three drop-ins per week.
- Include small perks — guest passes, workshop discounts, early booking.
- Remove friction from pausing instead of cancelling.

A member who can pause is far more likely to return than one forced to cancel.

## 4. Build belonging, not just attendance

People stay for community as much as the class. Learn names. Celebrate
milestones — a hundredth class, a teaching-training graduation. Host the
occasional non-class event. Students who feel *known* don't shop around on price.

## 5. Close the feedback loop

Quiet dissatisfaction is the most common reason members leave. Ask — briefly and
regularly — how things are going, and visibly act on what you hear. A member who
sees their suggestion turn into a schedule change becomes an advocate.

## The compounding effect

None of these levers is dramatic on its own. But retention compounds: a studio
that keeps 5% more of its members each year ends up dramatically larger and more
profitable than one that doesn't, with far less spent on acquisition. Pull these
levers consistently and growth starts to take care of itself.
