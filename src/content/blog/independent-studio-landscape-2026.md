What does your competition actually offer, and how is the field really
structured? We looked at **301 independent studios** across 32 cities and 9
countries to map the style mix and the market shape — the backdrop any
positioning decision is really made against.

> **First, the honest caveats.** This is *our own research and opinion*, not vendor-supplied or independently audited data. It's **directional, not definitive**: it reflects a curated sample of **301 studios across 32 cities and 9 countries**, which skews toward studios with a findable, English-language web presence — so read it as "where the wind is blowing," not a census. We verified each studio's *current* platform by following its live Book/Schedule link in June 2026, but software, pricing, and features change constantly. **Always confirm the current details on each vendor's own site before making a decision.** Company and product names are trademarks of their respective owners; we are not affiliated with, endorsed by, or speaking for any of them. None of this is legal, financial, or business advice.

In our reading, **Vinyasa + Hatha + Yin is the standard core** — offering it is
parity, not a point of difference. The genuine differentiators showing up in the
data are the **wellness layer** (sound-bath 9%, breathwork 6%) and **format-specific
hot** (roughly 23% run some heated/infrared format). **Pilates appears at 12%** — the
cross-sell a lot of studios are adding.

If you want a defensible position, the white space looks less like "another
Vinyasa schedule" and more like a distinctive **modality stack** — sound, breath,
infrared, Pilates cross-training — wrapped in an experience people can't get down
the street. (That's our opinion drawn from the pattern, not a guarantee; your
local market is the real test.)

## Market structure: who you're really up against

The field is overwhelmingly **owner-operated and single-location.** We counted
**252 distinct brands across 301 locations.** Only **29 brands** run more than one
location — so roughly **74% of the field is single-location independents.**

Multi-location consolidation is mostly a **US-metro move** — Black Swan (7), Yoga
Box in Denver (7), Flow in Austin (4), and a band of three-location brands behind
them. In destination markets, there are effectively **no chains at all.**

The translation for owners: in most cities your real competition is *other
owner-operated single studios*, not a chain — so the game is differentiation and
experience, not out-spending a franchise. In a few metros (Denver and Austin
stand out in our data), a handful of multi-location brands do set local pricing
and expectations, and it's worth knowing where you sit relative to them.

## If you take three things from this

1. **The core is parity.** Vinyasa/Hatha/Yin is table stakes; it won't set you
   apart on its own.
2. **Differentiate on modality and experience** — sound, breath, infrared, Pilates
   cross-training, and the feel of the place.
3. **Know your local structure.** Single-studio town or multi-location metro
   changes who you're benchmarking against and how you price.

None of this says there's one right way to run a studio — the opposite. The field
is wide open precisely because it's full of independents each doing their own
thing. Use this as a mirror for where you stand, then build the studio only you
can.
