# Teacher Compensation Guide

A practical guide for studio owners to set up, manage, and optimize teacher pay.

---

## Who This Guide Is For

This guide is for **studio owners and administrators** who need to:

- Set up pay structures for new teachers
- Evaluate whether their current pay models make sense
- Navigate common scenarios (raises, model changes, new hires)
- Understand the accounting and tax implications
- Export data for bookkeepers and payroll

If you're a teacher looking to understand your pay, see [Understanding Your Earnings](/teach/help/earnings).

## The Big Picture

### What You're Trying to Balance

Every studio owner faces the same fundamental tension:

**Studio sustainability** — You need to control costs, maintain margins, and build a business that can weather slow months.

**Teacher satisfaction** — Good teachers have options. Fair, transparent pay keeps them engaged and loyal.

**Alignment** — Ideally, when the studio does well, teachers do well. When classes are full, everyone wins.

There's no single "right" pay model—the best choice depends on your studio's stage, your local market, your class formats, and your relationships with teachers.

### The Core Principle

> **Pay should be predictable, transparent, and fair.**

Teachers should always be able to calculate their expected pay. Surprises—in either direction—erode trust.

## Pay Model Options

Tandava supports multiple pay models. Here's what each one means in practice.

### Per Class (Flat Rate)

**How it works:** Teacher receives a fixed amount for each class taught, regardless of attendance.

**Example:** $50 per class

**Pros:**
- Simple to understand and budget
- Predictable for both studio and teacher
- Easy to compare across teachers

**Cons:**
- No incentive to grow attendance
- Can feel unfair if classes vary wildly in size
- Doesn't scale with studio success

**Best for:** New studios, new teachers, classes with consistent attendance

---

### Hourly

**How it works:** Teacher receives a rate multiplied by the scheduled class duration.

**Example:** $40/hour × 1.25 hours = $50

**Pros:**
- Fair for classes of different lengths
- Familiar model (like other jobs)
- Easy to add setup/cleanup time

**Cons:**
- Doesn't account for class difficulty or attendance
- Same rate for a 30-student class and a 5-student class

**Best for:** Studios with varied class lengths, teachers who also do admin work

**Tip:** You can add setup and cleanup time (e.g., +15 minutes before, +10 minutes after) so teachers are compensated for arriving early and staying late.

---

### Revenue Share

**How it works:** Teacher receives a percentage of the revenue generated by their class.

**Example:** 30% of class revenue. If the class brings in $400, teacher earns $120.

**How class revenue is calculated:**
- Drop-in purchases for that class
- Prorated portion of class packs used
- Prorated portion of memberships (based on member's usage)

**Pros:**
- Aligns teacher and studio interests
- Teachers benefit from marketing and growth
- Rewards teachers who bring their own following

**Cons:**
- Pay varies week to week
- Can feel unstable for teachers who prefer predictability
- Requires trust in revenue calculation

**Best for:** Established studios, teachers with strong followings, workshop leaders

---

### Hybrid (Base + Revenue Share)

**How it works:** Teacher receives a guaranteed base pay plus a percentage of class revenue.

**Example:** $30 base + 10% of revenue. If class brings in $400, teacher earns $30 + $40 = $70.

**Pros:**
- Security of a base with upside from growth
- Encourages teachers to fill classes
- Limits studio's downside on slow days

**Cons:**
- More complex to explain
- Need to set both components appropriately

**Best for:** Studios in growth phase, experienced teachers, classes building momentum

---

### Tiered (Attendance-Based)

**How it works:** Teacher's per-class rate increases when attendance hits certain thresholds.

**Example:**
- 1-9 students: $40
- 10-19 students: $50
- 20+ students: $65

**Pros:**
- Directly rewards class growth
- Clear incentive structure
- Fair for high-variability classes

**Cons:**
- Can create pressure around threshold numbers
- Teachers may focus on quantity over quality

**Best for:** Studios optimizing for attendance, competitive markets

---

### Per Student

**How it works:** Teacher receives a fixed amount for each student who attends.

**Example:** $5 per student. If 18 students attend, teacher earns $90.

**Pros:**
- Perfectly fair for varying class sizes
- Clear, easy to calculate
- Strong growth incentive

**Cons:**
- Unpredictable pay
- Risk of very low pay on slow days
- May need a minimum guarantee

**Best for:** Hot yoga, high-volume studios, classes with 20+ average attendance

---

### Per Student + Base

**How it works:** Guaranteed base plus a per-student bonus.

**Example:** $25 base + $3 per student. If 18 students attend, teacher earns $25 + $54 = $79.

**Pros:**
- Security of guaranteed minimum
- Scales with attendance
- Balanced risk for both parties

**Cons:**
- Need to calibrate both components

**Best for:** Studios transitioning from flat rate to performance-based pay

---

### Bonuses

Any model can include **bonuses** for specific achievements:

**Attendance bonus:** +$25 when 20+ students attend
**Fill rate bonus:** +$25 when class reaches 80% capacity

Bonuses are great for:
- Rewarding exceptional performance without changing base rates
- Incentivizing growth in specific classes
- Celebrating milestones

## Choosing the Right Model

### By Studio Stage

#### Startup (First Year)

**Recommended:** Per class flat rate

**Why:** You're still learning your market. Costs need to be predictable. You can't afford pay that swings wildly.

**Typical rates:** $35-50 per class depending on market

**Path forward:** After 6-12 months, once you understand attendance patterns, consider moving star teachers to hybrid.

---

#### Growing (1-3 Years)

**Recommended:** Hybrid (base + small revenue share)

**Why:** You want teachers invested in growth, but you're still building. A modest base with 5-10% revenue share balances both needs.

**Example:** $35 base + 8% of class revenue

**Path forward:** As specific classes consistently fill, consider tiered or higher revenue share for those teachers.

---

#### Established (3+ Years)

**Recommended:** Tiered, revenue share, or per-student—depending on class type

**Why:** You understand your economics. High-performing teachers should share in success. You can afford performance-based models.

**Example portfolio:**
- New teachers: Per class ($45)
- Regular teachers: Hybrid ($35 + 10%)
- Star teachers: Tiered ($45 / $55 / $70) or 35% revenue share

---

### By Studio Type

#### Boutique Studio (1 location, <10 teachers)

Personal relationships matter more than complex incentive structures. Keep it simple.

**Recommended:** Per class or simple hybrid
**Focus on:** Fair base rates, annual reviews, relationship-based raises

---

#### Hot Yoga / High Volume

Class sizes vary dramatically (10 to 40+ students). Flat rates feel unfair.

**Recommended:** Per student or per student + base
**Example:** $20 base + $3/student
**Why:** Teachers filling 40-person classes deserve more than teachers with 12

---

#### Multi-Location

Different locations may have different economics. Downtown premium studio ≠ suburban community studio.

**Recommended:** Location-specific models
**Example:**
- Downtown: Hybrid with higher base
- Suburban: Per class with attendance bonus

---

#### Workshop / Training Focus

Specialty teachers often bring their own marketing and following.

**Recommended:** Revenue share (40-60%)
**Why:** They're essentially partners. Align incentives accordingly.
**Alternative:** Flat workshop fee negotiated per event

---

### By Teacher Experience

#### New Teachers (< 1 year teaching)

They're learning. Classes may be small. They need stability.

**Recommended:** Per class flat rate
**Rate:** Modest ($35-45)
**Review:** At 6 months, evaluate performance and discuss path

---

#### Experienced Teachers (1-3 years)

Proven ability. Starting to build following. Ready for growth incentives.

**Recommended:** Hybrid or low-tier tiered
**Example:** $40 base + 8% revenue
**Opportunity:** Add attendance bonus as carrot

---

#### Star Teachers (3+ years, strong following)

They fill classes. They could work anywhere. Keep them.

**Recommended:** Revenue share (25-40%) or aggressive tiered
**Key:** Make sure they earn more than market rate when classes are full
**Risk:** If they're unhappy, they'll leave—and take students with them

---

#### Guest / Visiting Teachers

One-off or occasional. Different relationship.

**Recommended:** Flat fee per workshop OR revenue share (50-60%)
**Why:** They're essentially contractors for a specific event
**Negotiate:** Each situation individually

## Setting Up Teacher Pay

### Step-by-Step

1. **Navigate to Staff Settings**
   - Go to `Settings → Staff`
   - Click on the teacher's name
   - Select the `Compensation` tab

2. **Choose the Pay Model**
   - Select from the dropdown: Per Class, Hourly, Revenue Share, Hybrid, Tiered, Per Student, Per Student + Base

3. **Configure the Rates**
   - Enter the specific values (rate in dollars, percentage, thresholds)
   - Add bonuses if desired

4. **Set the Effective Date**
   - Choose when this rate takes effect
   - For immediate: today's date
   - For future: the date when new rate should apply

5. **Add Notes (Optional)**
   - Document why this rate was chosen
   - Note any verbal agreements
   - Helpful for future reference

6. **Save and Review**
   - Click Save
   - System will show a preview of what the teacher would earn for a typical class

### Example: Setting Up Hybrid Pay

Let's say you're setting up Maya, an experienced teacher, with hybrid pay:

```
Model Type: Hybrid
Base Pay: $35.00 per class
Revenue Share: 10%
Attendance Bonus: $25 when 20+ students
Effective From: March 1, 2026
Notes: Promoted from per-class after strong Q4 performance
```

**What Maya will earn:**
- 12-student class, $300 revenue: $35 + $30 = $65
- 22-student class, $550 revenue: $35 + $55 + $25 bonus = $115

## Common Scenarios

### Giving a Raise

**Situation:** Teacher has been with you a year, attendance is up, you want to reward them.

**Steps:**
1. Go to their Compensation settings
2. Increase the rate OR switch to a better model
3. Set effective date (typically start of next pay period)
4. Add note: "Annual review - attendance up 25%"
5. Save

**Communication:** Tell them in person before they see it in the system. A raise should feel like recognition, not a surprise in their paycheck.

---

### Switching Pay Models

**Situation:** You want to move a teacher from flat rate to hybrid.

**Considerations:**
- Make sure the new model won't result in a pay cut during normal weeks
- Explain the upside potential clearly
- Consider a "floor" for the first 3 months while they adjust

**Steps:**
1. Calculate what they've earned recently under the current model
2. Design new model to match or slightly exceed that baseline
3. Set up new model with future effective date
4. Walk them through the change before it takes effect

---

### Handling a Slow Period

**Situation:** Attendance drops (summer, holidays). Teachers on per-student or revenue share are hurting.

**Options:**
1. **Temporary floor:** Guarantee minimum pay for the slow period
2. **Blended rate:** Switch to per-class during slow months
3. **Reduced schedule:** Fewer classes but maintained rate

**Communication:** Be proactive. Don't wait for teachers to complain. Acknowledge the reality and explain your approach.

---

### Dealing with Consistently Empty Classes

**Situation:** A class isn't filling. Teacher is earning full pay for 4 students.

**Options:**
1. **Cancel the class:** If it's not working, redirect the slot
2. **Move the time:** Maybe 6pm works better than 4pm
3. **Renegotiate:** Per-student model so economics make sense
4. **Marketing push:** Commit to promoting it for 8 weeks

**Don't:** Just cut the teacher's rate without addressing the underlying problem.

---

### New Teacher Probation

**Situation:** You're hiring someone new. Want to evaluate before committing to a high rate.

**Approach:**
1. Start with per-class at a modest rate ($35-40)
2. Set explicit review at 3 months
3. Document expectations: attendance, student feedback, reliability
4. If they meet expectations, upgrade to better model/rate
5. If not, have an honest conversation

## Contractor vs. Employee

### The Reality

Most yoga teachers are **independent contractors (1099)**, not employees (W-2).

This means:
- You don't withhold taxes—teachers handle their own
- No benefits, overtime, or minimum wage requirements
- Teachers have more schedule flexibility
- You must provide 1099-NEC forms for payments over $600/year

### Classification Matters

Misclassifying employees as contractors can result in IRS penalties. The key factors:

| Factor | Contractor | Employee |
|--------|------------|----------|
| Schedule | Teacher has flexibility | Studio sets schedule |
| Control | Teacher decides how to teach | Studio directs methods |
| Equipment | Teacher provides own (mats, music) | Studio provides everything |
| Exclusivity | Teaches at multiple studios | Works only for you |
| Pay structure | Per class or project | Hourly/salary |

**When in doubt:** Consult an employment attorney or CPA.

### Setting Up in Tandava

1. Go to `Settings → Staff → [Person] → Employment`
2. Select Worker Classification: Contractor or Employee
3. If Contractor: Collect and store W-9
4. System will handle 1099 reporting at year-end

## Tips and Bonuses

### Tips

Tandava tracks tips separately from base pay because:
- Tips have different tax treatment
- Students want to thank specific teachers
- Teachers want to see their tip earnings

**Configuration options:**
- 100% to teacher (default)
- Pooled among all teachers
- Custom split

**Important:** Some states have rules about tip pooling. Check your local laws.

### Bonuses

Two types of automatic bonuses:

**Attendance bonus:** Extra pay when attendance hits a number
- Example: +$25 when 20+ students attend
- Good for: Incentivizing class growth

**Fill rate bonus:** Extra pay when class reaches a capacity percentage
- Example: +$25 when class is 80%+ full
- Good for: Rewarding efficient use of space

Bonuses can be added to any pay model. They're calculated automatically based on check-in data.

## Front Desk and Hourly Staff

### Different from Teachers

Front desk staff typically:
- Clock in and out (not scheduled per class)
- Need overtime tracking
- May need break time compliance
- Are often employees, not contractors

### Recommended Approach

**Use external time tracking** like:
- Homebase (free for small teams)
- Deputy
- When I Work

These tools:
- Handle clock-in/out
- Calculate overtime
- Ensure labor law compliance
- Export directly to payroll (Gusto, ADP)

Tandava's strength is class-based compensation. For shift-based hourly work, purpose-built tools are better.

### If You Must Use Tandava

You can set up front desk staff with:
- Pay type: Hourly
- Schedule their "shifts" as pseudo-classes
- Pay is calculated based on scheduled hours

But this is a workaround, not a full time-tracking solution.

## Exports and Reporting

### Payroll Export

**Location:** Reports → Payroll → Export

**Options:**
- **Summary:** One row per teacher with totals
- **Detailed:** One row per class with full calculation breakdown
- **By format:** CSV, Excel, Gusto-compatible, ADP-compatible

**What's included:**
- Teacher name and ID
- Pay period dates
- Classes taught
- Pay model used
- Base pay, bonuses, tips
- Total amount

### For Your Bookkeeper

Your bookkeeper needs to know:
- Total payroll expense by category
- Tips pass-through (liability, not expense)
- Contractor payments for 1099 preparation

**Monthly export:** Summary by teacher and pay type
**Year-end export:** Total payments per contractor for 1099s

### Reports to Review Monthly

1. **Payroll by Teacher:** Who's earning what?
2. **Cost per Class:** Are some classes losing money?
3. **Revenue Share Analysis:** Are share percentages sustainable?
4. **Attendance vs. Pay:** Is pay aligned with performance?

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How often should I review pay rates?

Annually at minimum. More often for high-performers you want to retain. Create a calendar reminder.

### What if a teacher asks for a raise?

Listen. Ask what they think is fair and why. Compare to market rates and their performance. Don't say no immediately—even if the answer is no, take time to consider it seriously.

### Should I share pay information between teachers?

That's your call. Some studios are fully transparent; others keep individual rates private. Whatever you choose, be consistent. Teachers talk—inconsistency breeds resentment.

### What's a fair revenue share percentage?

Typical range: 25-40% depending on experience and who handles marketing. Workshop leaders often get 50-60%.

### How do I handle a teacher who's underperforming?

Separate conversations: feedback about performance, then discussion of pay implications. Don't use pay cuts as punishment—address the issue directly first.

### What if I can't afford to pay competitive rates?

Be honest. Some teachers will stay for non-financial reasons (community, schedule, growth opportunity). Others won't. You need teachers who want to be there.

### Should I pay more for harder classes?

Maybe. Hot yoga is physically demanding. Advanced workshops require expertise. If a class requires more from the teacher, consider reflecting that in pay.
